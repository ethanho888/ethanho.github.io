For those struggling in CS

Getting into Berkeley Engineering has been a highlight of my life, what I didn’t know was that it was going to be my only win for a while. When I walked into my first EECS course I felt way out of my league — the large class sizes made me feel isolated and I felt like I was being left behind by my peers. The glorification of my major made me feel inadequate in comparison and I began to question why I was EECS at all.

“I regret to inform you” is the phrase I’ve read the most during my college career. At first it hurt a lot. I have a spreadsheet with a hundred rows that details every internship and organization I have applied for. When rejection is coupled with exhaustion, it hurts. It was hard to watch my friends get into student organizations and find new communities from the sidelines. When rejection is coupled with gate-keeping, it hurts more. I’ve also been on the other side, trying to decide someone’s worth by some essays and a resume. When rejection is coupled with guilt, it can hurt too.

However what I have taken away from years of rejection as a Berkeley engineer is that there are other ways of showing how you can add value. Personal relationships are valued a lot more than I realized. I was under the impression that engineering roles were purely about technical skill, but like any other job, it is crucial to be friendly and sociable. You would be surprised how much you can learn when you're not afraid of reaching out.

Things will work out if you keep your chin up. It's ok to feel like the worst engineer in the room, as long as you know how to escape that mindset. You are your own biggest advocate. Not even the most accomplished engineer can completely overcome rejection, it’s NP-hard.
